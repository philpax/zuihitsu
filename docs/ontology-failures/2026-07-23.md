# Ontology failures, surveyed 2026-07-23

One survey in the dated series recording where the current ontology fails in practice, kept as
evidence for the planned redesign (a future agent's ontology, designed against these failures
rather than against theory alone). Each failure names its class and links the concrete evidence —
an event-log span, an eval run, or an archaeology write-up — so the redesign proposal can be
checked against every failure, case by case. Later surveys get their own dated documents in this
folder; a mitigated failure stays recorded, since a mitigation in the current ontology is itself
evidence of the workaround tax the redesign should erase.

## The maintenance-passes campaign survey

Gathered while building the maintenance passes and the attestation model, and from a full survey of
the testing instance's knowledge base (103 live entries at the time of the survey).

### Facts are sentences

The root failure, from which most of the others follow. An entry is a prose blob anchored to
exactly one memory, so every downstream mechanism re-derives structure from prose — dedup and
consolidation through embedding geometry, arbitration through model judgement, temporal placement
through date extraction — paying the derivation repeatedly, nondeterministically, and fallibly.
Consolidation's output is a new sentence whose relationship to its sources is recoverable only
through provenance metadata, never through structure. A structural question ("what did this person
build?") has no structural answer; the only read is search-plus-prose.

Evidence: the maintenance passes themselves are the workaround tax, and the campaign's bugs were
all re-derivation bugs — an inverted dendrogram cut merging everything above cosine 0.15 (caught by
the `consolidation_preserves_privacy` scenario, fixed on this branch), and the recurrence
mis-extraction below.

### One event, one subject, many copies

Entries bind to a single memory, so an event with several participants shatters into per-subject
re-phrasings. The flagship case: one implementation-event (one person built a bot in two days after
another shared an architecture) was recorded three times in a single session — once per
participant, in subject-appropriate phrasing — beside the shared topic that already abstracted it.
Two of the three copies were written in one flush turn, confirming the scaffold's own warning that
duplication is worst at the seams. Mitigated on this branch by a write-time cross-subject advisory
and flush/scaffold teaching (Scaffold v27, Flush v4); the mitigation steers the agent around the
representation rather than fixing it.

Evidence: the testing instance's log, seqs 436–440 (the first copy, filed on the bot) and 590–595
(the flush burst: the two re-filings plus the topic mint).

### Relations are bare edges

A link carries a relation name, a posture, and nothing else — no attributes, no validity interval.
"Worked at X from 2019 to 2021" cannot be a relation, so it becomes prose, and re-enters the
facts-are-sentences failure. Relation vocabulary is agent-coined strings with declared cardinality
but no hierarchy or domain constraints, so coinage drifts: the same semantic relation has been
coined four ways (event-to-place) across sessions with nothing structural to prevent a fifth.

### Schedule and description conflate in the temporal model

The bitemporal split (`asserted_at` versus `occurred_at`) is sound, but a single per-entry
occurrence field carries two meanings: when the fact's referent happens, and when the agent should
act. The extraction stamped `FREQ=DAILY` on a fact *describing another bot's daily context
seeding*, and the agent thereafter woke for someone else's cron job at every boot. Mitigated by the
extraction's third-party-routine rule (TemporalExtraction v6) and the occurrence-withdrawal event;
the conflation itself remains.

Evidence: the testing instance's log — the entry carrying `FREQ=DAILY` and a `ScheduledJobFired`
for it at every boot until the occurrence was withdrawn; also the namesake's date (1789) stamped
onto a fact about the bot itself, the different-referent failure predating the v5 rule.

### Identity is binary and entangled with storage

`same_as` classes with a designated primary work, but identity is merge-or-stranger: no partial or
context-dependent identity, no credence on a merge, and the class machinery is complex precisely
because identity resolution is entangled with where facts are stored. Issue #94 records the deeper
finding: knowledge-overlap adjudication is unsound in principle (knowledge can be recited), and the
promising direction — revocable composite identity with assumption-stamped derivations — is a
truth-maintenance discipline the ontology does not yet have anywhere.

### Relation schemas are immutable and vocabulary drifts

Once registered, a relation's schema — inverse, cardinality, symmetry, description — is fixed
forever (#42): a mis-registered relation cannot be amended, only abandoned, which compounds the
coinage drift above. Schema evolution for relations does not exist.

### Identity complexity leaks into behaviour

Beyond the binary-merge model itself, the machinery's complexity shows up as agent behaviour
failures: after a confirmed cross-platform merge, the agent failed to relay a sibling stub's
history back to its own teller in 7 of 10 runs (#104, `a_merge_lands_and_memory_unifies` at 0.30 on
the relay metric) — the visibility and retrieval machinery held, and the agent's own model of the
merged identity is what faltered.

### Belief has no credence model

Attestation (this branch) records who stands behind a fact and lets a fact outlive one teller's
retraction — real progress — but arbitration remains episodic model judgement recording a prose
note, contradictions persist as flags rather than being revised, and nothing represents how
strongly the agent believes anything.

### Hygiene thresholds are embedder geometry

Every similarity threshold (dedup 0.95, consolidation 0.85/0.75) is a constant in one embedding
model's cosine space. Measured on real data: the same fact re-phrased sat at 0.966 and 0.851
(caught at 0.85) and 0.757 (missed until the threshold moved); the same text under two different
subject prefixes sat at 0.80 (short text) versus 0.94 (long text) under the test embedder. An
embedder change invalidates all of these silently; the re-embed machinery exists but no threshold
recalibration does.

### Load-bearing behaviour is prompt-sensitive

The corroboration-capture rate moved from roughly 6% to 75% of eligible cases on a one-sentence
scaffold change (v26). That a data-capture behaviour swings that far on phrasing is the welding
failure in miniature: the interface between the live neural judgement and the symbolic store is
behavioural, tuned by wording, and re-tunes itself unpredictably across model generations.

Evidence: the `attestation_hidden_confirmation` eval, 1/16 capture across two runs before the
scaffold change, 6/8 after.

### The neural writer is unverified

The model is the sole writer of structure. Guards check authority and visibility, never truth: a
confidently recorded falsehood meets no check until a contradiction happens to collide with it in
arbitration. Judgement provenance records which model and template produced a derivation
(`produced_by`) but not the evidence or criteria, so the neural half of the system is materially
less auditable than the symbolic half.
